What Makes a Generic Medicine Safe?

For a generic medicine to be legally sold in India, the manufacturer must prove to CDSCO that:

  • Same active ingredient ‚Äî identical molecule, identical dose
  • Bioequivalence ‚Äî the generic must release the same amount of drug into the bloodstream at the same rate as the original
  • GMP manufacturing ‚Äî factory must meet WHO Good Manufacturing Practice standards
  • Quality testing ‚Äî each batch tested for purity, potency, and dissolution
  • Proper labeling ‚Äî correct dosage, indications, contraindications

These are the same standards as branded medicines. A generic company cannot cut corners on active ingredients or manufacturing standards.

5 Common Myths About Generic Medicines ‚Äî Busted

MYTHFACT
"Generic medicines are of lower quality"
Quality is not determined by brand name. It's determined by manufacturing standards and active ingredient purity. CDSCO-approved generics meet identical quality standards. The "lower quality" perception comes from cheaper packaging, not lower medicine quality.
MYTHFACT
"Generic medicines work slower or less effectively"
Bioequivalence testing proves that generics achieve the same blood levels as the original at the same time. By definition, they cannot work "slower" if bioequivalent. Any perceived difference is usually psychological (placebo/nocebo effect).
MYTHFACT
"Cheap medicine = poor quality medicine"
Price reflects R&D recovery, patent costs, and marketing spend ‚Äî not quality. A 10-year-old molecule with expired patents can be made cheaply by many manufacturers without any quality compromise. Paracetamol cost ‚Çπ0.30 per tablet ‚Äî it's not worse because it's cheap.
MYTHFACT
"Doctors don't use generics themselves"
Surveys show that when doctors in developed countries take medicines themselves, they overwhelmingly choose generics. In the US, 90% of prescriptions are filled with generics. Indian government doctors prescribe generics routinely.
MYTHFACT
"Generic medicines have more side effects"
Side effects come from the active ingredient ‚Äî which is identical in generic and branded. The inactive ingredients (fillers, colourants) are different but generally inert. In extremely rare cases, someone might react to a specific filler ‚Äî but this is rare and unrelated to quality.

When to Exercise Extra Caution

While generics are safe for 95%+ of medicines, a small group called Narrow Therapeutic Index (NTI) drugs require extra care. These medicines have a narrow window between effective and toxic doses, so even minor variations matter. Always consult your doctor before switching these:

  • Warfarin (blood thinner)
  • Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Valproate (anti-epilepsy)
  • Levothyroxine (thyroid)
  • Digoxin (heart)
  • Lithium (psychiatric)
  • Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus (immunosuppressants)

For these, switching is fine ‚Äî but your doctor should monitor blood levels during the transition.

The Global Evidence

  • WHO's Essential Medicines List consists primarily of generics
  • India's AIIMS and major government hospitals use generics exclusively

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if a generic medicine is CDSCO approved?
Check the medicine packaging for "Mfg. Lic. No." (Manufacturing License Number) and "Approved by CDSCO" indication. You can also verify on the CDSCO website. Buying from licensed pharmacies (online or offline) ensures you get approved medicines.
Why do some generic medicines look different from branded ones?
Appearance (colour, shape, size) is not patented ‚Äî different manufacturers make their tablets look different. This has no effect on how the medicine works. Only the active ingredient content matters.
My doctor says not to switch to generic ‚Äî what should I do?
Some doctors have concerns about specific NTI drugs or have other clinical reasons for branded prescriptions. Ask your doctor to explain why. If satisfied with the reason, follow their advice. If it seems like there's no medical reason, you can seek a second opinion.
